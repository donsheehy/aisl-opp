Greatest Common Divisor

Summary

The greatest common divisor $\gcd(a, b)$ of two integers $a$ and $b$ (not both zero) is the largest positive integer that divides both. Part of Number Theory.

  • Definition and basic properties
    • We say $d = \gcd(a, b)$ if $d \mid a$, $d \mid b$, and every common divisor of $a$ and $b$ also divides $d$.
    • The gcd is symmetric, associative, and satisfies $\gcd(a, 0) = |a|$ and $\gcd(a, b) = \gcd(|a|, |b|)$.
    • Two integers are coprime (or relatively prime) when $\gcd(a, b) = 1$.
  • Relation to the least common multiple
    • The least common multiple $\mathrm{lcm}(a, b)$ is the smallest positive integer divisible by both $a$ and $b$.
    • For nonzero integers, $\mathrm{lcm}(a, b) \cdot \gcd(a, b) = |ab|$.
  • Euclidean algorithm
    • The Euclidean algorithm computes $\gcd(a, b)$ by the recurrence $\gcd(a, b) = \gcd(b, a \bmod b)$, terminating at $\gcd(d, 0) = d$.
    • Correctness rests on the fact that the set of common divisors of $a$ and $b$ equals the set of common divisors of $b$ and $a \bmod b$.
  • Extended Euclidean algorithm
    • The extended Euclidean algorithm additionally returns integers $x, y$ satisfying $ax + by = \gcd(a, b)$.
    • These coefficients certify Bézout's identity and yield the modular inverse of $a$ mod $n$ when $\gcd(a, n) = 1$ (see modular_arithmetic).

Bézout's identity: for any integers $a$ and $b$, not both zero, there exist integers $x, y$ such that \[ ax + by = \gcd(a, b). \] Moreover, $\gcd(a, b)$ is the smallest positive integer expressible in the form $ax + by$.

Euclid's lemma: if a prime $p$ divides a product $ab$, then $p \mid a$ or $p \mid b$. More generally, if $\gcd(a, n) = 1$ and $n \mid ab$, then $n \mid b$.
