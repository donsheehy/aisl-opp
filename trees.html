Trees

Summary

A tree is a connected, acyclic graph. Part of Graph Theory.

  • Characterizations of trees
    • A graph $G$ on $n$ vertices is a tree if and only if it is connected with $n-1$ edges, or equivalently acyclic with $n-1$ edges, or equivalently has a unique path between every pair of vertices.
  • Rooted and binary trees
    • A rooted tree designates one vertex as the root, inducing parent–child relationships.
    • A binary tree has each node with at most two children; it has at most $2^h$ leaves at depth $h$.
  • Spanning trees and minimum spanning trees
    • A spanning tree of $G$ is a tree subgraph containing every vertex of $G$.
    • A minimum spanning tree (MST) minimizes total edge weight and can be computed by Kruskal's or Prim's algorithm.

For a graph $G$ on $n$ vertices, the following are equivalent: (1) $G$ is a tree; (2) $G$ is connected and has $n-1$ edges; (3) $G$ is acyclic and has $n-1$ edges; (4) every two vertices are connected by a unique path; (5) $G$ is connected and removing any edge disconnects it; (6) $G$ is acyclic and adding any edge creates a unique cycle.
