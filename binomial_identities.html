Binomial Identities

Summary

Binomial identities are equalities relating binomial coefficients, often proved combinatorially by counting the same set in two ways. Part of Combinatorics.

  • Pascal's identity and Pascal's triangle
    • Pascal's identity states $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k}$.
    • Pascal's triangle is the array of $\binom{n}{k}$ generated by this recurrence.
  • Binomial theorem
    • The binomial theorem expands $(x + y)^n$ as a weighted sum involving binomial coefficients.
  • Vandermonde's identity
    • Vandermonde's identity relates $\binom{m+n}{r}$ to a sum of products of smaller binomial coefficients.
  • Combinatorial proofs
    • A combinatorial proof (or double counting argument) establishes an identity by counting the same set in two different ways, without algebraic manipulation.

Pascal's identity: $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k}$.

Binomial theorem: $(x + y)^n = \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^k y^{n-k}$.

Vandermonde's identity: $\binom{m+n}{r} = \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{r} \binom{m}{k}\binom{n}{r-k}$.
