Mathematical Induction

Summary

Mathematical induction is a family of proof_techniques for establishing statements indexed by the natural numbers or other well-founded sets. Part of Logic and Proofs.

  • Weak (ordinary) induction
    • Prove $P(0)$ (base case) and $P(n) \rightarrow P(n+1)$ (inductive step) to conclude $\forall n \in \mathbb{N}, P(n)$.
  • Strong induction
    • In the inductive step, assume $P(0), \ldots, P(n)$ collectively to prove $P(n+1)$.
    • Strong induction is logically equivalent to weak induction but is often more convenient when $P(n+1)$ depends on more than just $P(n)$.
  • Structural induction
    • Apply induction on the recursive structure of objects such as trees, lists, or formulas.
    • The base case handles atoms; the inductive step handles each constructor.
  • Well-ordering principle
    • Every nonempty subset of $\mathbb{N}$ has a least element.
    • The well-ordering principle is equivalent to ordinary induction over $\mathbb{N}$.
